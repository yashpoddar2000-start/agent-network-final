At $27.4M revenue: rent is 7.3% of revenue.
At $15M revenue: rent becomes 13.3% of revenue.

Same rent payment. But it consumes nearly double the revenue as a percentage.

This pattern repeats across every fixed cost: utilities, insurance, management overhead. Higher revenue spreads these costs over a larger base. Each dollar of fixed cost becomes proportionally cheaper per sale.

That's fixed cost leverage.

But revenue doesn't equal profit:

Restaurant-level operating margin (industry estimates): 12-15%

At $27.4M, Din Tai Fung generates ~$3.3-4.1M profit per location.

Why margins stay at 12-15% despite massive revenue: hand-folded dumplings and full-service dining eat into margins that drive-thru or counter-service models can preserve.

The fragility of high-volume models:

At 475 parties per day, fixed costs sit at 10-12% of revenue. The model prints money.

Drop to 300 parties per day a 37% decline in traffic and those same fixed costs jump to 16-19% of revenue. Margins compress toward zero.

The rent doesn't change. The utilities don't change. The insurance doesn't change. But revenue drops, so fixed costs become a larger percentage of what's left.

Large footprints in prime locations mean fixed costs don't flex down when volume drops. You either run at capacity and win, or run at 60% and bleed.

Din Tai Fung built a model that's incredibly profitable when busy and fragile when it's not. Fixed costs are a weapon when you have volume.
